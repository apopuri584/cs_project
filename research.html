Research & Data Sources

Our visualizations are grounded in real environmental data, chosen to highlight how air quality varies across the United States and how human activity shapes these patterns.

Data Source 1: Industrial Waste & Air Quality

We identified this data source while researching different factors that influence air pollution, with industrial waste emerging as one of the most significant contributors. To make this data more interpretable, we used the Air Quality Index (AQI) and state-level data to create a choropleth map, allowing users to easily compare air quality across different regions of the United States. This visualization could help individuals with asthma or heart conditions identify areas with better air quality. Additionally, policymakers and lobbyists can use this information to guide decisions and develop policies aimed at reducing air pollution. We also used the AQI and AirQualityIndustrialToxinConcentration_2024 columns to create a scatter plot with a best-fit line, which highlights the relationship between industrial waste and air quality. This visualization helps show how increases in industrial waste are associated with higher AQI values, and it can be useful for scientists studying pollution trends as well as lawmakers working to limit industrial emissions. Finally, we created box plots using the same variables to examine the distribution and outliers in both AQI and industrial waste levels. These outliers highlight specific states that may require further investigation, helping policymakers identify areas where pollution levels are unusually high or low and take targeted action.

Data Source 2: AQI Changes During COVID-19

We discovered this data source while exploring changes in air pollution over time, with a particular focus on how the COVID-19 pandemic impacted the Air Quality Index (AQI). Using the Micrograms per Cubic Meter (PM2.5) column and the AQI column from Data Source 1, we created a side-by-side bar chart that allows for a direct comparison of AQI values for each state in 2020 and 2025. This visualization provides valuable insights for lobbyists and policymakers, showing how pandemic-related policies, such as quarantines, influenced air pollution levels.

Data Source 3: Long-Term AQI Trends (1990–2025)

We also discovered this data source while examining changes in air pollution over time. It provides the lower, upper, and mean population-weighted Air Quality Index (AQI) for each year from 1990 to 2025. Using the Year and Exposure Mean columns, we created a line graph to illustrate how AQI values have changed over time in the United States. This visualization can help historians and environmental scientists analyze pollution in a historical context, and it can also assist lobbyists and policymakers in understanding how different government policies have influenced air quality over the years.

Data Source 4: Sources of Air Pollution

We found this data while investigating the different factors that influence AQI values in the United States. It shows the percentage contribution to air pollution from various sectors and their associated pollutants. Using the Sector Source and Percent Contribution columns, we created a bar chart with Sector Source on the x-axis and Percent Contribution on the y-axis. We considered using a pie chart, but the wide range of contribution values made a bar chart a more effective choice for visualizing the data.
